A protein called immune checkpoint plays an important role in regulating the immune system. The immune checkpoints that are activated in cancer suppress the antitumor activity of the immune system, thus suppressing the immune response against the cancer cells. A number of immune checkpoint inhibitors have been developed as cancer therapies based on the study of these proteins and pathways. Antibodies blocking the immune checkpoint molecule CTLA-4 resulted in successful anti-tumor immune responses in murine cancer models, according to preliminary preclinical studies. In addition, studies targeting the PD-1/PD-L1 pathway followed.
